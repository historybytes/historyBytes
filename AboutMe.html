About Me

Karen Latimer

It started with Ada Lovelace.

During her Bachelor of Education, Karen Latimer came across the story of a 19th-century mathematician who wrote what is now recognised as the world's first computer program — more than a century before the computer existed. She was brilliant, she was visionary, and she had been almost entirely left out of the story Karen had been told about computer science.

That discovery sparked a question that has quietly shaped everything Karen has done since: how many more have we missed?

Karen is a high school CS teacher at an independent school in Manitoba, Canada, where she teaches students from Grade 9 through Grade 12. She's also a sessional instructor in a Bachelor of Education program, helping train the next generation of CS teachers. Her research focuses on CS education and algorithmic bias — the places where the technology we build reflects the blind spots of the people who built it.

Over the years, her CS program has grown into something she's proud of: a deliberate, equity-centred curriculum that doesn't just teach students how to code, but asks them to think critically about who codes, who has historically been excluded, and why it matters. Senior students facilitate CS workshops at an under-resourced middle school. The history of the field — its pioneers, its politics, its overlooked contributors — is woven into every course.

History Bytes is the next chapter of that same work, just with a wider audience. Each episode digs into the human story behind a moment in computing history — the decisions, the rivalries, the breakthroughs, and the people who made them. Especially the ones history almost forgot.

Karen hosts the show from Manitoba, where she lives with her family. She is a published researcher, a conference presenter, and someone who still gets genuinely excited every time she finds a name that should have been in the textbook and wasn't.

And that's her story.
